Housing (Barely)

HOUSING CRISIS Fall 2024 — Present

Freshman Enrollment Spikes 43%; Triples Become Quads, Offices Become Dorms

After a surprise enrollment surge, the university discovered that an abandoned Army base does not, in fact, have enough beds. Doubles became triples. Triples became quads. Sophomores were kicked off campus. Gavilan Hall — a former office building — is being converted into housing. Students now live where spreadsheets used to live. One resident describes Gavilan Hall as "a paperwork penitentiary with a microwave." The housing waitlist peaked at 300+ names, and the university switched to a queuing system that students have compared to Ticketmaster — you get a place in line, watch the little bar move, and then it sells out while you're verifying you're not a robot. "I've tried for housing three semesters in a row," says junior Marissa B. "I've gotten better seats at a Taylor Swift concert. And she has more housing."

VANDALISM April 2025

Otter Statue Vandalized 11 Days After Unveiling — Suspects Cited "Housing Crisis"

The brand new 9-foot bronze otter statue — unveiled April 17 to a crowd of 500 — was found wrapped in toilet paper and tagged with black ink reading "fix housing" and "money hungry fucks" just 11 days later. The administration called it "a disappointing act of vandalism." Students called it "the most honest feedback the university has received all year." The statue has since been cleaned and its nose is back to being rubbed for exam luck, but it's rumored to flinch whenever someone mentions the housing portal. "I don't condone vandalism," said senior Taylor K., "but that otter gets it."

OTTER CYCLE CENTER Free Stuff

Free Bike Rentals Because Walking Across a Former Army Base Is Too Much

The Otter Cycle Center offers free bike rentals, free repairs, group rides, and bike storage. It's almost like the university knows the campus is spread across 1,000 acres of decommissioned military infrastructure. Students can borrow a bike, ride to class, and pretend they're in a Netflix coming-of-age film — right up until the fog rolls in and they hit a curb.

Housing Options Breakdown

PREMIUM Promontory

Promontory — The Promised Land

Full kitchen. Washer and dryer in the unit. Private bathroom. It's essentially off-campus luxury living, but technically on campus and priced accordingly. Students who live here never let anyone forget it. "I have a full-size fridge," says resident Taylor K. "I'm not saying I'm better than you. But I am."

STANDARD Residence Halls

Main Campus — Where the Magic (and Mold) Happens

The traditional dorm experience: shared bathrooms, community kitchens, and a mysterious patch of mold in Yarrow Hall that has its own Instagram following. Rooms range from doubles to "surprise!" quads. The walls are thin enough that you'll learn your neighbor's schedule, music taste, and relationship status without ever meeting them.

EXILE East Campus

East Campus — Free Parking, But at What Cost?

East Campus offers apartment-style living with free parking — but it's a 20-minute walk (or 10-minute bike ride) from the main campus. Residents describe it as "living in a different zip code." Pros: you have a kitchen. Cons: you'll need a weather forecast to know if you should bring an umbrella to class. (Spoiler: it's always foggy.)

NEW Gavilan Hall

Gavilan Hall — Formerly an Office, Now Your Home

In response to the housing crisis, the university converted a former administrative office building into student housing. Rooms are spacious by office standards — meaning you get a cubicle-sized living space with a bed instead of a desk. The building still has conference rooms, which students have repurposed as study lounges. "I signed up for a triple and got a former HR department," says one resident. "The vibes are... corporate."

IRONY Ongoing

Across the Street: The Dunes — $1M Homes for Retirees, While Students Sleep in Triples

Right across from campus, a brand new neighborhood called "The Dunes" features luxury homes starting around $1 million. They have ocean views, modern kitchens, and enough bedrooms for a family of four. Meanwhile, on campus, the university is converting office cubicles into dorm rooms and asking students if they'd be willing to triple up in spaces designed for single occupancy. "I look at those houses from my triple's window," says one student. "I can see the kitchen. I think about what it would be like to have my own counter space. One day." The irony is not lost on anyone except, apparently, the developers.
